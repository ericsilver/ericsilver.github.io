The Price of Absence

July 15, 2026 · first draft

A couch is one of the last bargains in America. The emptiness around it is not.

For most of history the relationship ran the other way. Objects were dear — a chair was a craftsman's week, a coat was a season's wages — and space was the cheap part. Wealth therefore displayed itself as accumulation: plate on the sideboard, books behind glass, flesh on the body. The poor were marked by absence — bare rooms, bare shelves, thin frames.

Manufacturing inverted the price signs, and the display inverted with them. Objects now cost almost nothing, and everything that surrounds an object — the floor beneath it, the room that organizes it, the services that maintain it, the discipline that refuses it — has become expensive. So the signs traded places: the bare counter, the empty calendar, and the flat stomach now read as money, while the crowded apartment and the heavy body read as its lack. Nothing moral changed. Prices changed.

The couch costs $500; the floor under it costs $5,000

Start with the arithmetic of furniture. IKEA will sell you a three-seat sofa (the SALTSJÖBADEN) for $499. It is 80¾ inches wide and 34⅝ deep: about 19.4 square feet of floor. The median American home currently lists at about $226 per square foot, so the patch of house directly beneath the sofa is worth roughly $4,400 — nine times the sofa. And a sofa does not live on its footprint alone. Interior-design clearance conventions want 18 inches to the coffee table and a 30–36 inch walkway along its length; grant the sofa its working perimeter and it claims 45–60 square feet, call it $10,000–$13,500 of median-priced housing. In Manhattan, where rent runs about $7.90 per square foot per month, the same sofa-plus-clearance is roughly $400 a month, every month, forever.

This is not an artifact of bubbly asset prices. Even at raw construction cost — the National Association of Home Builders puts new single-family construction at $162 per square foot in 2024 — the floor under the sofa costs six times the sofa. And the two assets move in opposite directions: the sofa depreciates to a curb, while the space appreciates, because most of what a house is worth is now land. Knoll, Schularick, and Steger's 145-year price history finds that rising land prices account for about 80 percent of the global house-price boom since World War II; construction costs explain little. The manufactured part of shelter behaved like other manufactures. The unmanufacturable part is what got expensive.

A junk drawer makes the same point at smaller scale. Its contents — dead batteries, takeout menus, three chargers for departed phones — might fetch $20. Its share of the cabinet's floor claim is a square foot and change: call it $300 of house wrapped around $20 of stuff, a 15-to-1 ratio of container to contained. The garage runs the ratio at scale. UCLA's Life at Home project, which inventoried 32 middle-class Los Angeles households room by room, found that 75 percent of garages could no longer hold a car; a two-car garage is about 400 square feet, or $90,000 of median-priced structure serving as a mausoleum for objects worth a fraction of that.

America built double the house and still rents 2 billion square feet of overflow

The stock of dwelling space exploded. The average new American house grew from roughly 983 square feet in 1950 to about 2,150–2,200 today, while the average household shrank; living space per person in a new home nearly doubled between 1973 and 2015, from about 507 to about 1,000 square feet. Objects filled it anyway, then overflowed. The overflow became an industry: American self-storage now spans roughly 2.1–2.6 billion rentable square feet across some 60,000 facilities — more locations than McDonald's, Starbucks, and Subway combined — collecting over $40 billion a year. In 1995 about one household in seventeen rented a unit; today it is roughly one in ten. The average unit runs $121 a month, which is to say that millions of households pay apartment-grade rent — storage runs $1.20–$1.80 per square foot per month, about what the average American apartment costs per square foot — for rooms no person lives in. Consumer Reports once worked out that a typical unit's rent, invested instead, would compound to $11,000 in five years, more than the resale value of what most units hold.

Inside the house, the numbers are softer but point the same way. An industry survey last year (by a storage marketplace, so season to taste) found a majority of Americans ceding 100–500 square feet of their homes to rarely used possessions, with a fifth ceding more than 500 square feet — six figures of real estate at national prices. A tracker-fob vendor's survey puts the time spent hunting lost objects at 2.5 days per person per year, with $2.7 billion spent annually replacing things that exist but cannot be found. Vendor surveys both — but the direction survives the discount: the marginal cost of an object is now trivial, and the carrying cost of objects in aggregate is not.

An organized home is purchased, not willed

The tidy home reads as a virtue of character, which is exactly how expensive signals prefer to be read. In practice order is capital-intensive. It requires slack square footage — the closet with room to spare, the garage that holds only cars — and it requires a service layer: the organizer, the junk hauler, the donation run (which assumes a car), the storage unit that launders clutter into invisibility for $121 a month. Above all it requires the confidence of replacement. Discarding a cable, a coat, or a crib is cheap if you can rebuy on demand; if you cannot, keeping everything is not a pathology but an inventory policy. The epidemiology agrees: hoarding severity runs inverse to household income, and in the UCLA households, mothers' cortisol profiles tracked how cluttered they described their homes as being. The stress of stuff is real, measurable, and unequally distributed — and one household's clutter is another's rationally held reserve against a $40 replacement it cannot casually make.

Marie Kondo's advice is free. Following it is not. “Does it spark joy?” is a question you can only afford to ask about objects you can afford to lose.

Thinness has a price list

The body followed the living room. Corpulence signaled wealth for the same reason a full sideboard did — calories were scarce — and the association inverted when calories became the cheapest manufactured good of all. The pattern is old enough to have a canonical citation: Sobal and Stunkard's 1989 review found obesity tracking high status in poor countries and low status in rich ones, most strongly among women. Avner Offer put the mechanism plainly: “under affluence, it is slimness that is difficult, and demonstrates a capacity for self-control.”

The American numbers: among women, obesity runs 45.2 percent below 130 percent of the poverty line against 29.7 percent above 350 percent; by education, 40 percent among adults with high school or less against 27.8 percent among college graduates. (Honesty requires the caveats: the income gradient is steep for women, flat for men overall, and actually runs the other way for non-Hispanic Black men. The education gradient is the robust one.)

The price structure underneath is not subtle. Adam Drewnowski's food-price work finds vegetables cost about five times what refined grains and fats cost per calorie ($0.68 versus $0.14 per 100 kcal); in his Seattle supermarket audit the least energy-dense fifth of foods cost $18.16 per 1,000 calories against $1.76 for the densest fifth — a tenfold spread — and the healthy basket was inflating while the dense one got cheaper. A Harvard meta-analysis prices the healthiest diet pattern at about $1.50 a day over the least healthy — $550 a year per person, real money at the bottom of the distribution and rounding error at the top. Notably, when economists tested the fashionable supply-side story — food deserts — with supermarket entries and household moves, the food environment barely moved diets; the gap is mostly prices, time, and knowledge, not distance to a Kroger. Calories are cheap everywhere. It is their absence that varies in price.

Exercise runs on the same ledger, and the time-use data contain a trap for the lazy version of this essay. Low-income Americans do not lack free minutes — they have more of them (386 versus 339 minutes a day for men) — yet do half the leisure exercise (women: 9 versus 17 minutes a day). What is scarce is not clock time but the complements that convert time into exercise: safe streets, childcare, sleep between shifts, and disposable income for the paid layer — the gym, the league, the lessons. The complements gradient is steep: 34.8 percent of adults below the poverty line meet federal aerobic guidelines against 66.8 percent at six times the line, and families now spend $1,016 a year on a child's primary sport, up 46 percent since 2019, with the rich-poor participation gap widening to 20 points.

Then came the purest product ever offered in this market: a drug whose deliverable is absence of appetite. GLP-1 agonists list at $1,086–$1,349 a month, or $299–$449 cash direct from the manufacturers. Medicare has excluded weight-loss drugs by statute since 2006 (a $50-copay pilot began this month); as of last year only 13 state Medicaid programs covered them for obesity. In KFF's polling, 56 percent of users found them hard to afford, and among the insured, above-median-income patients had 60 percent higher odds of being on semaglutide at all. Thinness was already priced like a luxury; now it is literally one, with a monthly subscription and an income-graded uptake curve.

Everything you can drop on your foot got cheaper; everything done for you got dearer

The pattern generalizes far past couches and calories, and the general version has a famous picture: Mark Perry's “chart of the century,” tracking American prices from 2000 to mid-2022. Overall CPI rose 74 percent. Televisions fell 97 percent; toys fell 72 percent; furnishings and clothing were flat in nominal terms, meaning steep real declines. Meanwhile hospital services rose 220 percent, college tuition 178 percent, childcare 115 percent. Quality-adjusted, durable goods prices fell about 2.8 percent per year for a quarter century; the BLS's quality-adjusted new-car price rose 2.4 percent in total between 1995 and 2019. Everything tradeable, automatable, and containerizable deflated. Everything made of present human attention inflated.

The economics is William Baumol's, from 1967: when manufacturing productivity soars and a string quartet still takes four musicians, the quartet's relative price must rise, because its labor is bid away by the productive sectors. Helland and Tabarrok's book-length audit of health and education inflation concludes it is mostly this effect — not administrative bloat, not greed newly discovered. The homely version sits in any kitchen: a Le Creuset Dutch oven — the finest cooking vessel a household is likely to own, warrantied for life — costs about $435. Dinner for two at The French Laundry runs $800–$1,800: two to four Dutch ovens, consumed in an evening. Down-market the ratio holds; the average restaurant meal costs $16.28 per serving against $4.23 at home. The pan is a manufacture, so it is cheap and lasts decades. The meal is an ephemeral service, so it costs more than the capital that could produce a thousand of it. We have reached the point where the instrument is disposable relative to the performance.

One honest complication: over that same 2000–2022 window, average wages rose 99.6 percent — faster than CPI. This is a relative-price story, not an immiseration story. But relative prices are what class is made of. When goods deflate and services inflate, ownership stops discriminating — everyone has the flatscreen — and consumption of human attention starts: the trainer, the tutor, the therapist, the tasting menu, the organizer. The rich and poor increasingly own the same objects and consume utterly different verbs.

More of the economy is collected rather than made — and more of us are employed doing the collecting

Zoom all the way out and the same inversion appears in the national accounts. Labor compensation peaked at 58.1 percent of GDP in 1970 and sits at 51.1 percent in 2025. The mirror-image line — corporate profits plus rental income of persons — bottomed at 4.2 percent of GDP in 1986 and has since more than tripled, to 15.1 percent; the after-tax corporate profit share posted its second-highest quarterly reading since 1947 earlier this year. Housing services alone — actual rents plus the rent homeowners implicitly pay themselves — run about 12 percent of GDP, and recall that the appreciating ingredient is land, the one input nobody produced. A definitional caution is owed: not every dollar of profit is “rent” in the economist's sense of unearned scarcity income, and the split is genuinely contested — Stansbury and Summers read the labor-share decline as rents migrating from workers to capital rather than monopoly rising. But the direction is not contested: a growing slice of national income accrues to holding things — land, housing, claims, intellectual property — rather than to making things.

Income that accrues to holding must be administered, and that shows up in the occupational tables. Employment in finance, insurance, and real estate grew from 3.8 percent of nonfarm payrolls in 1947 to 5.8 percent today — and because its pay is high, its value-added share is far larger: finance and insurance alone went from 2.8 percent of GDP in 1950 to over 8 percent, and the full FIRE complex now approaches 21 percent. America fields about 1.28 million security guards — more than its high-school teachers, and nearly double the 1980 count — plus 466,000 property managers, 418,000 compliance officers, and one lawyer per 250 residents where 1960 made do with one per 700. Bowles and Jayadev's broader accounting of “guard labor” — work that defends claims rather than producing anything — puts it at roughly a fifth of the employed workforce (a quarter, if prisoners and the unemployed are counted in, as their broad measure does). Roughly one working American in five is paid to fence, file, adjudicate, insure, or watch — the maintenance staff of the holding economy. The self-storage facility, staffed by a manager guarding objects nobody visits, on land appreciating faster than anything inside, is the perfect miniature.

Wealth as absence

Assemble the pieces and the era's signature becomes legible. Production made objects nearly free; what cannot be produced — land, present human attention, and the state of not-having — is what the price system now rations. So wealth migrated into absences. The expensive home is the empty one. The expensive body is the one calories visibly passed over. The expensive meal vanishes in two hours; the expensive drug deletes an appetite; the expensive service leaves no object at all. The poor household, priced out of every one of these subtractions, holds its wealth the only way wealth can still be held cheaply: as objects — and is then read, by people who mistake prices for character, as undisciplined.

Our poverty statistics were built for the old sign convention — they count what households have, and by that count material poverty looks nearly solved: the poor own televisions, sofas, phones. Counted in absences — empty square feet, foregone calories, unclaimed hours, defended quiet — the gradient is as steep as it ever was, and steepening, because absence is precisely the good that manufacturing cannot cheapen and that a holding economy charges ever more to provide. Globalization made everything cheap except nothing. Nothing has never cost so much.
