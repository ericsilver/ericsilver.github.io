The Tails a Tribe Needs

July 20, 2026 · draft

There is a habit in the study of rationality of scoring one person against a scoreboard built for one person. You take an individual, you hand them a decision, you compare what they did to what a lone utility-maximizer with unlimited time and a probability textbook would have done, and you record the gap as a defect. The gap gets a name — sunk-cost fallacy, loss chasing, mood-congruent judgment — and the name implies a repair. The whole apparatus assumes that the thing being optimized, and the thing that could be broken, is the individual.

But the individual is rarely the unit that natural or cultural selection was working on. A band of hunters is not thirty independent optimizers; it is one foraging portfolio that happens to be distributed across thirty bodies. And a portfolio has a property no single decision has: it can be better off holding positions that any one holder should rationally close. Once you take that seriously, a surprising number of our catalogued “biases” stop looking like errors in the person and start looking like the tails of a distribution the group has reasons to keep wide.

Sunk cost keeps the group's bets spread

Consider the hunter who keeps working a technique that is no longer paying, or the forager who returns to a patch that has thinned out, long past the point where the expected return justifies it. Individually this is the sunk-cost fallacy in its purest form: throwing good effort after bad because of what has already been spent.

Now watch the same behavior from above the tribe. If every member updated perfectly and abandoned the underperforming approach the instant it dipped below the current best, the entire band would converge on one method — and would be blind the moment the environment shifted and made yesterday's loser today's winner. What keeps the group covered is exactly a supply of members who won't update, who stay committed to the thinning patch and the unfashionable technique out of something that, examined one skull at a time, looks like stubbornness. Philip Kitcher made this argument formally for scientific communities: a field is better off when some researchers doggedly pursue approaches that are, for them individually, the wrong bet, because the community needs its risk diversified even at the cost of individual rationality. Evolutionary biology calls the same structure diversified bet-hedging. The sunk-cost “fallacy” may be one of the mechanisms that installs it in a human group — a psychological glue that guarantees the distribution of effort never collapses to a single point.

The bias, in other words, is not in the hunter. It is in the yardstick, which was built to grade one hunter when the thing being graded is the spread.

Mood tracks the state of the world, not a flaw in the person

The same shift rescues mood from the language of malfunction. We tend to describe mood that moves with circumstance as a leak — comfort lifting us, hardship dragging us down, as if a well-built mind would hold steady. But mood keyed to conditions is doing a job. Low mood after a run of losses lowers the threshold for reward-seeking and raises it for risk; it pulls an animal toward caution and conservation precisely when the environment has signaled that the returns are poor. Daniel Nettle and others have argued that this is what mood is for — a conserved mechanism for retuning behavior to the recent state of the world. Comfort-tracking is not the system failing to be stoic. It is the system working.

And here the portfolio view adds something the individual account leaves out. A group does not only need each member's mood tuned to conditions; it needs a range of tunings across members at any moment — some easily discouraged and thrown into caution, some hard to move and still probing when everyone else has pulled back. A band in which every temperament responded identically to the same signal would lurch as one body, over-committing and over-retreating in unison. The spread of moods is itself the hedge.

The high-variance move looks like a bias only from above the line

Depression's link to risk-taking reads as pathology compounded — a person already impaired now also gambling. But there is a threshold logic that makes the gamble the correct play. When resources fall below the floor that basic needs require, the expected-value calculation inverts: with little left to lose, the high-variance option dominates the safe one, because only the long shot can clear the bar. The behavior looks reckless against an average, and adaptive against a threshold. Recent work finds exactly this V-shape — risk-taking that falls as people approach comfort and rises again as they drop below subsistence. What we file under impaired judgment is, part of the time, judgment that has correctly noticed the safe path no longer reaches anywhere worth going.

Anomie as the group sampling its own edges

The hardest case, and the one our vocabulary handles worst, is anomie — the drift into normlessness, the loosening of the individual from the shared rules. Durkheim treated it as a social pathology, a sign that the collective order had failed to hold someone. The portfolio view suggests a less tidy possibility: that a society which bound every member tightly to its current norms would have no way to discover that its norms had gone stale. The anomic member — detached, unpersuaded, no longer performing the consensus — is, at the level of the group, a probe sent to the edge of the strategy space. Most such probes find nothing and read, individually, as loss and worthlessness. But a group needs some fraction of its members standing outside the agreement at any time, or it cannot tell an agreement that still works from one that merely persists. This is the least established of the cases and the easiest to push too far; I raise it as the frontier of the idea, not its safe ground.

What the frame does and does not claim

None of this says the stubborn hunter feels no waste, the depressive no pain, the anomic no despair. The costs are real and land on real individuals; a portfolio that benefits from its tails is no comfort to the position being held at the tail. The claim is narrower and only about naming. When we call a behavior a bias, we are asserting that it departs from what optimization would produce — and we have quietly fixed the optimization at the level of the single mind. Move the level up, to the band that forages together and rises and falls together, and some of what looked like defect resolves into design: the mechanisms by which a group keeps its bets spread, its moods varied, its edges manned. The bias was never only in the person. Some of it was in the scoreboard.
